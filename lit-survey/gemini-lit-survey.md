Theoretical and Empirical Foundations of Metric Geometry in Large-Scale Embedding SpacesThe transition of natural language processing and computer vision from symbolic and heuristic-based architectures to dense, high-dimensional representation learning has necessitated a rigorous mathematical understanding of embedding space geometry. Embedding spaces in Large Language Models (LLMs) and Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are not merely passive repositories of features; they are structured manifolds where the distance and orientation of vectors dictate the semantic alignment, reasoning capability, and generalization of the model. This report provides a systematic survey of the literature regarding distance and metrics in these spaces, examining the foundational geometry, similarity functions, evaluation frameworks, cross-modal alignment, model similarity, contextual semantic distance, and downstream implications for robustness and fairness.Foundational Geometry of Embedding SpacesThe global and local structure of embedding spaces provides the first layer of interpretability for transformer-based architectures. Modern research suggests that instead of utilizing the full dimensionality of the provided vector space, model representations often exhibit a high degree of anisotropy, clustering within narrow angular regions commonly referred to as the "cone effect". This concentration leads to the "modality gap" in VLMs and "dimensional collapse" in LLMs, where the effective rank of the representation matrix is significantly lower than its nominal dimensionality.The phenomenon of anisotropy in LLMs is particularly pronounced in the hidden states of deeper layers. While early layers might show a degree of isotropy to capture varied linguistic features, subsequent layers tend to project tokens into a narrow cone, where even semantically unrelated tokens share high cosine similarity. This collapse is often attributed to the ReLU non-linearities and the softmax objective, which encourage representations to concentrate on a low-dimensional subspace spanned by a few principal directions. Theoretical explorations into the manifold hypothesis suggest that these high-dimensional data points are actually samples from a low-dimensional manifold embedded in the high-dimensional space. While cosine similarity is the de facto standard for LLMs and VLMs, recent research has highlighted its severe limitations in high-dimensional, anisotropic spaces. Cosine similarity focuses purely on directional alignment, discarding the vector magnitude which often encodes critical information such as word frequency, semantic certainty, or informativeness.For tasks requiring robust out-of-distribution (OOD) detection, the Mahalanobis distance is often preferred as it incorporates the covariance structure of the data, allowing the model to distinguish between semantic variance and true anomalies. In scenarios involving hierarchical data, such as taxonomic relations or robotic predicate trees, Euclidean and angular metrics suffer from "hierarchical collapse." Hyperbolic distance metrics, specifically within the Poincaré ball or Lorentz models, are increasingly employed to embed tree-like structures with low distortion due to the exponential volume growth of hyperbolic space. In RAG systems, the choice of distance metric (cosine vs. Euclidean) directly influences the fairness and robustness of the system. "Adversarial hubs"—perturbed inputs that act as nearest neighbors to a vast number of unrelated queries—can be used to inject spam or misinformation into search results. First is the conflict between Anisotropy and Expressivity. While anisotropy (the cone effect) is often framed as a "bug" that limits retrieval, some evidence suggests it is an inherent property of learning the hierarchical and directional structure of language. The goal may not be a perfectly isotropic space, but a "controlled anisotropy" that aligns with task requirements.Second is the tension between Platonic Convergence and Architectural Fingerprints. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis suggests a grand convergence as models scale, yet metrics like Unified Topological Signatures (UTS) reveal that models retain distinct "fingerprints" based on their activation functions and training objectives. This suggests that while models might agree on the "what" (semantics), they differ significantly on the "how" (geometry).Finally, there is a mismatch between Synthetic Metrics and Downstream Utility. The field is rapidly moving away from simple distance rankings (e.g., R@K) toward more nuanced measures like task-centric recall and metamorphic fairness scores. The "Information Loss Funnel" illustrates that optimizing for a distance metric in isolation is insufficient for building robust production systems.Gap Analysis: Underexplored Research FrontiersDespite the breadth of current research, several critical gaps remain:Metric-Aware Architecture Search: Most foundations models use standard LayerNorm and Softmax, which drive anisotropy.
